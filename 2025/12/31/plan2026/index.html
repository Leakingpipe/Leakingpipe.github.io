2026 Annual Plan

¶ Prologue

2025 went by pretty fast. Looking back, the list of changes was sureal: I received offers from computer science programs as a fine art student, interned at the National Center for Applied Mathematics, finally got out of an abusive relationship, and gained another 4k followers on social media.

Yet, amidst these milestones, I was hit by waves of self-doubt. It seemed like all of my friends were moving on to a new stage of their lives - getting married, having kids, etc.

“What am I doing? Why am I doing this?” I kept on asking myself. Then I saw this quote a friend sent me:

“You are too focused on the future without realizing that today is exactly what you prayed for years ago.”

That sentence hit me hard. It unlocked a buried memory.

In 2017, a young man was learning to code on freeCodeCamp. Back then, I didn’t really understand what I was looking for. I was fascinated by the tools I used in 3D softwares, and I vaguely knew “code” built them. But with limited information, I mistakenly thought web development was the foundation of “coding”. So there I was, struggling through English documentation, trying to learn things that everyone told me were “too hard for an art student.”

I bet that young man never imagined that, years later, he would be holding an offer letter for an MSc in Computer Science.

Between then and now, I picked up scattered pieces of knowledge—calculus, linear algebra, Python, shader, and C++—mostly through MOOCs and books. But I never truly thought I could apply them professionally. I was a master’s student at Beijing Film Academy; the “logical” path was to become an animation artist, just like all my seniors.

The turning point came in September of 2024. As graduation approached, I felt a sense of urgency. knew I have burried my thoughts for years but now it’s my final chance:if I don’t do this now, I probably won’t have the chance to do it before retirement.

I asked a friend to shed some light on where to start. He sent me some papers and source code . At first, I was confused. But as I parsed through the math and logic, I was thrilled. I realized the people who built these tools were geniuses. I told my friend: “Dude, this is what I’ve always been looking for.” It wasn’t just coding; it was the engine behind the art.

After months of grinding, I applied for the CS program in March and received the offer in April. It was a surprise because, on paper, I was an outsider. But in reality, I had been preparing for this transition for years without even knowing it.

Now, I have a new goal. I refuse to trap myself in the kind of self-doubt that wastes time. As I start my first annual plan for 2026, I am writing this down as a reminder.

Whenever I feel anxious, I will look up this page and tell myself: Don’t worry. Just do your thing. Because the life you have now is exactly what you prayed for years ago.
